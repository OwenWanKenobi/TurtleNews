The oldest turtle stories rarely arrive as complete skeletons. More often, they come as fragments: a shell plate, a jaw, a footprint, or a fossil bed that asks scientists to read slowly.

That slow reading is exactly why turtle archaeology has become such a good fit for Turtle News. The beat connects evolution, climate, geography, and human curiosity. A single shell can point toward ancient wetlands, migration routes, predator pressure, or changes in temperature that shaped entire habitats.

Modern reporting on these discoveries also benefits from restraint. The best fossil stories do not need to overpromise. They explain what researchers found, what remains uncertain, and why a small anatomical clue can still matter.

The redesigned Science section will make room for that range: fossil finds, conservation research, sea turtle nesting data, and field notes from the edge of the archive. The theme is simple enough. Shells remember more than they first appear to.
